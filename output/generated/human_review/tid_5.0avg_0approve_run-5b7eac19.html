2. Technical Invention Disclosure

2.1 Problem Statement

Parallel metadata-heavy allocation paths in journaled Linux filesystems often examine many free-space candidates while holding per-group or per-allocation-domain protection. In ext4, this appears in buddy-based extent allocation where multiple candidates may be compared against requested length, cluster size, alignment, stripe geometry, goal distance, locality, reservation pressure, and fragmentation. The expensive part is often heuristic comparison, not the final metadata update. Keeping those comparisons under lock increases contention, inflates lock hold time, worsens p99 latency, and reduces throughput on large x86 servers. A useful solution must move only advisory scoring outside the lock while preserving journal ordering, delayed-allocation correctness, reservation accounting, and native allocator authority.

2.2 Prior-Art Gap

Existing ext4 mballoc summaries, XFS per-AG accounting and hinting, and Btrfs free-space caches each maintain allocator summaries and may perform partial rechecks, but they do not define all of the following together: a bounded by-value snapshot taken under the native lock into preallocated owned storage only; a fixed-shape feature pack suitable for interchangeable scalar, AVX512, and AMX backends; a mutation-sensitive tuple with enumerated per-filesystem bump sites; deterministic top-K replay and tie-breaking; no-pointer candidate identities resistant to ABA and recycled IDs; formal monotonic-safe pre-discard rules limited to impossible-fit conditions; bounded retry and time budgets; and a reusable cross-filesystem adapter API. Generic machine-learning or heuristic file-system optimization literature also does not address kernel-context restrictions, xstate handling, journaling lock order, or proof obligations around stale-state invalidation.
