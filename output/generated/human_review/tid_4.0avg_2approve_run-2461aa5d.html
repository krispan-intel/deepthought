2. Technical Invention Disclosure

2.1 Problem Statement

Linux networking can only be safely optimized from x86 PEBS samples if the kernel can prove that a precise sample belongs to the exact RX poll or TX submission execution slice that was live on the interrupted CPU when the sampled microarchitectural event occurred, and can also prove that the sampled identity cannot alias a NAPI instance or TX queue object that has since been disabled, deleted, reset, resized, rebound, or index-reused. The hard problem is therefore not collecting PEBS, but constructing a correctness contract that survives delayed PMIs, task preemption, task migration, PREEMPT_RT threaded softirq execution, object lifetime churn, and CPU hotplug. Existing per-CPU cookie ideas are insufficient because PEBS delivery is not guaranteed to occur before a context switch. A sample triggered while task A is inside `__napi_poll()` or `__dev_queue_xmit()` may be delivered after task A has been preempted, after task B is running on the same CPU, or after task A has migrated. If the kernel merely snapshots a per-CPU cookie or uses current task and current queue state heuristically, it can misattribute a stale sample to an unrelated execution slice and then drive the wrong actuation decision. Generic perf attribution is adequate for reporting, but not for in-kernel policy changes that alter RPS/XPS masks, NAPI budgeting, GRO timing, or queue-level backpressure. Object lifetime is the second correctness barrier. `napi_id`, queue index, and `ifindex` are not non-aliasing lifetime keys. They can be reused across `napi_disable()` and `napi_enable()`, `netif_napi_del()` and re-add, queue-count changes, `ndo_stop()` and `ndo_open()`, PCI AER reset, devlink reload, driver unbind and bind, and device unregister and re-register. A sample that names only NAPI ID or queue index can therefore point at a logically different object after reset or reuse. NMI context cannot repair this by chasing pointers into `napi_struct`, `net_device`, driver rings, or task structures because those objects may already be quiesced or freed. The invention must also be safe under nested PMIs and CPU hotplug. A PMI can arrive while already inside the overflow handler; any design that reserves a ring slot and later completes it without an explicit commit protocol risks exposing partially written records. A final PMI can also arrive during CPU offline; therefore PEBS-visible buffers cannot be freed with normal per-CPU teardown timing. Finally, because PEBS latency and data-source fields are sensitive telemetry, the actuation path must be privilege-gated, namespace-scoped, rate-limited, and fail-closed so that cross-tenant side-channel amplification or adversarial mis-steering does not occur.

2.2 Prior-Art Gap

Four nearby bodies of prior art exist, but none teaches the claim boundary above. First, Linux seqcount, seqlock, and `seqcount_latch` patterns solve stable snapshot publication, yet they do not solve delayed-PEBS attribution. A latch can tell an NMI reader whether one payload copy is self-consistent, but it says nothing about whether that payload still belongs to the currently running task slice after scheduler switch-out or migration. A POSA could combine seqcount with a per-CPU network cookie, but would still lack the scheduler-coupled mirror and drop-on-task-switch acceptance rule that makes stale PMIs fail closed instead of being heuristically reused. Second, existing perf and PEBS attribution patterns are observational, not actuation-safe. `perf_event` and x86 PEBS support record IP, PID/TID, data source, latency, callchains, and optionally physical address, and userspace tools or eBPF programs can aggregate those signals. That prior art is sufficient to say that some CPU or task experienced a memory stall in a networking path, but it does not teach a kernel-internal acceptance contract that validates the sampled tuple against a live NAPI or TX sidecar before changing network policy. It also does not teach a scheduler-fast-path mirror that is intentionally updated on switch-in and switch-out to invalidate stale attribution. Third, Linux networking already contains tuning mechanisms such as RPS, RFS, XPS, BQL, NAPI weight, GRO flush timing, and driver-private adaptive moderation. Those mechanisms act on queues, flows, or devices, but they rely on software counters, IRQ rates, queue depth, or driver heuristics; they do not consume Intel PEBS data-source and latency telemetry, and they do not solve the attribution and lifetime safety problems required to use PEBS for closed-loop actuation. Likewise, ad hoc generation counters occasionally appear in drivers, but prior art does not provide an enumerated invalidation matrix covering the exact lifetime transition points in net core and driver reset paths. Without that matrix, 'add a generation counter' is obvious but incomplete, because mixed `{id, generation}` tuples can still race with queue reinitialization and reuse. Fourth, existing perf sample rings and generic lockless logging designs do not satisfy the NMI and hotplug requirements here. They often assume normal interrupt context, allow retry loops, or rely on consumer progress to reclaim state. This invention instead requires a per-possible-CPU spill ring with one PMI writer, explicit commit visibility, immediate drop of recursively nested PMIs, drop-new on full buffers, and persistence across CPU offline so that no final PMI ever targets freed storage. The combination of scheduler-mirrored task publication, enumerated network lifetime bumps, and nested-NMI-safe commit semantics is therefore not taught by prior seqcount usage, prior perf attribution, or prior networking tuning frameworks. Strategically for Intel, the gap matters because PEBS is the Intel-specific precision mechanism that makes network-path memory stalls actionable without always-on tracing overhead. Prior art leaves PEBS as a diagnosis tool. The invention turns it into a safe kernel optimization primitive for Intel server networking stacks, especially where Xeon platforms and Intel Ethernet adapters dominate latency-sensitive cloud, edge, and telco deployments.

2.3 Proposed Invention

The invention comprises five integrated components. 1. Task-scoped publication descriptors. Each task that enters an instrumented RX or TX span owns a `struct pebs_net_pub` descriptor referenced by `task_struct->active_net_pub`. The descriptor contains only immutable or sidecar-stable fields needed by the PMI path: publication kind (RX or TX), net namespace cookie, device identity, queue or NAPI unique ID, current generation, flow hash or socket cookie if available, owner task pointer, owner CPU, current run-sequence, and timestamp metadata. Entry and exit helpers update this descriptor only in short non-sleeping windows; the descriptor is never dereferenced in NMI outside its own double-buffered publication storage. 2. Scheduler-mirrored run-state with drop-on-task-switch semantics. The scheduler maintains a per-CPU `pebs_sched_mirror` record containing `{seq, current_task, active_pub, run_seq}`. On every switch-out and switch-in, the scheduler writes the mirror in the fast path under the runqueue lock. If the next task has `active_net_pub != NULL`, the scheduler assigns a new `run_seq`, mirrors the pointer, and republish-updates the descriptor so that the live slot carries the current CPU and run-sequence for that execution slice. If the current task is switched out, the mirror is cleared. The PMI acceptance rule is exact: accept only if the mirror snapshot is stable, `current == mirror.current_task`, `mirror.active_pub == current->active_net_pub`, the publication snapshot is stable, `snapshot.valid == 1`, `snapshot.owner_task == current`, `snapshot.owner_cpu == raw_smp_processor_id()`, `snapshot.run_seq == mirror.run_seq`, and the referenced sidecar generation is still the current even live generation. Any mismatch means drop. This is the concrete drop-on-migration and drop-on-task-switch rule that prior per-CPU cookies lack. 3. Enumerated lifetime-bump matrix for RX and TX objects. Every PEBS-visible object gets a stable unique ID and a separately bumping generation. `napi_uid` and `txq_uid` are allocated from per-netns `atomic64_t` allocators so that namespace scope is explicit and `ifindex` reuse is irrelevant. Generation transitions are explicit: (a) `netif_napi_add()`, `netif_napi_add_tx()`, and `netif_napi_add_config()` allocate sidecar, assign a fresh `napi_uid`, and set `napi_generation = 2` before publish; (b) immediately after `napi_disable()` returns, bump to the next odd generation and mark quiesced; (c) immediately before `napi_enable()` makes the instance schedulable again, bump to the next even generation and republish live bindings; (d) `netif_napi_del()` bumps to odd if needed, removes the lookup mapping, then completes `synchronize_rcu()` before free; (e) `netif_set_real_num_rx_queues()` bumps all affected RX-side NAPI or queue sidecars to odd before topology change and to new even values only after the replacement bindings are fully installed; (f) `ndo_stop()` after `napi_disable()` and `netif_tx_disable()` completion bumps all affected RX and TX objects to odd before ring free; (g) `ndo_open()` after ring allocation and queue-to-NAPI binding, but before enabling NAPI or waking TX queues, bumps affected objects to new even generations; (h) reset and rebind flows, including `ndo_tx_timeout()` recovery, PCI AER `reset_prepare` and `reset_done`, devlink `reload_down` and `reload_up`, and driver queue reinitialization paths, must call the same odd-before-teardown and even-after-republish helpers. Devices that do not wire these helpers are fail-closed for actuation. 4. NMI-safe spill ring. Each possible CPU owns a permanently allocated ring of slots `{commit_seq, payload}` plus producer and consumer cursors. The PMI path is the only producer for its CPU. On entry, it increments a per-CPU nesting counter; if the depth is not one, it increments a nested-drop statistic and returns immediately. For depth one, it computes `next = prod_seq + 1`; if `next - cons_seq > ring_size`, it increments a full-drop counter, leaves the ring unchanged, decrements nesting, and returns. Otherwise it writes payload bytes into the selected slot, then performs `smp_store_release(slot->commit_seq, next)`, then `WRITE_ONCE(prod_seq, next)`. The consumer in process context accepts a slot only if `smp_load_acquire(slot->commit_seq) == expected_seq`; incomplete records are invisible. Policy on full ring is drop-new, preserving already committed history. Buffers live in per-possible-CPU memory and are not freed on CPU offline; offline only disables local sampling and marks the CPU quiescing. 5. Safe Intel-specific actuation. Accepted records are drained into a kernel worker that groups by live `{netns_cookie, device_uid, object_uid, generation, flow tuple, PEBS data source, PEBS latency band}`. Actuation is permitted only when enabled by `CAP_NET_ADMIN` in the target namespace, when the tuple survives repeated samples over a hysteresis window, when the target device advertises helper coverage for required generation bumps, and when rate limits and rollback timers allow changes. Permitted actions are bounded to reversible network controls such as RPS/XPS CPU masks, GRO flush timeout, NAPI budget hints, IRQ affinity hints, or BQL thresholds. This exploits Intel PEBS precision as a closed-loop optimization primitive while remaining safer than userspace perf daemons or blind driver heuristics.

2.4 Architecture

The architecture has four runtime objects and two key algorithms.

Runtime objects: (1) `struct pebs_net_pub` per active task publication, containing an `epoch` and two publication slots; (2) `struct pebs_sched_mirror` per CPU, containing `seq`, `current_task`, `active_pub`, and `run_seq`; (3) RCU-stable sidecars `struct pebs_napi_sidecar` and `struct pebs_txq_sidecar`, each storing unique ID, current generation, namespace cookie, device cookie, queue index, and enabled/covered bits; and (4) a per-possible-CPU spill ring with commit-sequenced slots.

Scheduler mirror algorithm: on switch-out or switch-in, the scheduler is the sole writer of `pebs_sched_mirror`. It performs `WRITE_ONCE(seq, old + 1)` to mark an update in progress, writes `current_task`, `active_pub`, and `run_seq`, then performs `smp_store_release(seq, old + 2)`. If the incoming task has `active_net_pub != NULL`, the scheduler also republish-updates that descriptor's inactive slot so that `owner_cpu` and `run_seq` match the current CPU slice before the task returns to execution. The NMI reader executes: `seq0 = smp_load_acquire(mirror->seq)`; if `seq0` is odd, drop; copy `task`, `pub`, `run_seq`; `seq1 = smp_load_acquire(mirror->seq)`; if `seq0 != seq1`, drop. There is no spin in NMI; instability means immediate discard.

Publication latch algorithm: the task entry helper or scheduler switch-in helper is the sole writer of a given `pebs_net_pub` at that instant. To publish a live state, the writer selects the inactive slot `idx = (epoch ^ 1) & 1`, writes all payload fields into that slot in ordinary program order, including `valid`, `owner_task`, `owner_cpu`, `run_seq`, object IDs, generations, namespace cookie, and timestamps, then performs `smp_store_release(pub->epoch, epoch ^ 1)`. To clear the publication, the writer fills the inactive slot with the same identity fields but `valid = 0`, then flips `epoch` with `smp_store_release`. The NMI reader executes `e0 = smp_load_acquire(pub->epoch)`, copies the selected slot, then executes `e1 = smp_load_acquire(pub->epoch)` and accepts only if `e0 == e1`. `valid` is not used as the ordering primitive; the release on `epoch` orders the entire slot, so weakly ordered architectures cannot observe a mixed payload from different epochs.

Hook points and context assumptions are explicit. RX publication begins immediately before invoking the driver's poll work from `__napi_poll()` and ends immediately after the poll returns. TX publication begins on the path from `__dev_queue_xmit()` through queue selection to `ndo_start_xmit()` and ends when the submission span returns. The short publication update itself is non-sleeping. On mainline softirq paths, existing softirq non-preemptibility suffices; on PREEMPT_RT or threaded NAPI paths, the design remains correct because scheduler mirroring invalidates stale visibility on switch-out, so the publication does not need to remain globally visible while another task runs. NMI code never dereferences unstable `net_device`, `napi_struct`, or driver-ring pointers; it only reads the mirror, the publication slot, and RCU sidecars keyed by stable IDs.

CPU hotplug handling is fail-safe. Per-possible-CPU rings and mirrors are allocated when the feature is enabled and freed only after all PEBS events are disabled and an RCU grace period completes. CPU offline performs: disable local PEBS event; mark the CPU quiescing with release semantics; clear the scheduler mirror; and drain the ring from a housekeeping CPU. A late PMI during offline either appends a final committed record into still-live storage or drops because the mirror is absent; it never writes into freed memory.

2.5 Implementation Plan

Phase 1: Introduce core data structures and static keys in x86 perf and scheduler code. Add `task_struct->active_net_pub`, `struct pebs_sched_mirror`, per-possible-CPU spill rings, and the NMI acceptance path in the x86 PEBS overflow handler. Wire scheduler switch-in and switch-out updates in the context-switch fast path behind a static key so that non-enabled systems pay no cost. Phase 2: Add RX and TX publication helpers and sidecars. Hook RX entry and exit around `__napi_poll()` and TX entry and exit around `__dev_queue_xmit()` to `ndo_start_xmit()` submission spans. Create sidecar allocators keyed by per-netns atomic64 unique IDs and publish RCU lookup tables for NAPI and TX objects. Add coverage bits so actuation is automatically disabled until the driver or core path has complete helper wiring. Phase 3: Implement the exact generation-bump matrix. Add helper wrappers for `netif_napi_add*()`, post-`napi_disable()` quiesce, pre-`napi_enable()` republish, `netif_napi_del()`, `netif_set_real_num_rx_queues()`, `netif_set_real_num_tx_queues()`, `ndo_stop()`, `ndo_open()`, and reset/rebind helpers for AER, devlink reload, and timeout recovery. Ensure the odd-before-teardown and even-after-republish transitions are serialized by existing RTNL or device state locks; where a path lacks that serialization, add a required driver callback wrapper and mark the device uncovered until it is adopted. Phase 4: Add the drain worker and bounded actuation layer. Implement grouping, hysteresis, namespace and capability checks, rollback timers, and a minimal reversible actuation set such as XPS/RPS mask updates, NAPI budget hints, IRQ affinity hints, and BQL threshold nudges. Export coverage, drop counters, and per-device enable state through debugfs or sysfs. Phase 5: Upstreaming strategy. Split the work into reviewable series: (1) x86 PEBS core plus spill ring; (2) scheduler mirror; (3) networking sidecars and lifetime helpers; (4) policy and actuation layer; and (5) documentation and selftests. This framing isolates the Intel-specific PEBS core from broader networking policy debate while preserving the full claim boundary.

2.6 Validation Plan

Correctness validation starts with memory-order proofs. Add LKMM litmus tests for the scheduler mirror update protocol and the publication latch protocol, proving that an NMI reader cannot accept mixed task/pub/run-sequence or mixed epoch payloads under weak ordering. Add KCSAN and lockdep-assisted stress tests to verify that no NMI reader dereferences unstable pointers and that sidecar generation comparisons remain race-free under queue resize and reset. Delayed-PMI attribution validation uses forced scheduler churn on Intel Xeon systems with PEBS enabled. Run packet workloads that repeatedly trigger RX and TX spans while simultaneously forcing `sched_setaffinity()` migrations, `sched_yield()` preemption, cgroup CPU throttling, and PREEMPT_RT threaded softirq execution. Acceptance criterion: zero accepted samples whose `owner_task`, `owner_cpu`, `run_seq`, or sidecar generation fail a post-hoc trace correlation. False drops are acceptable and measured; false accepts are not. Lifecycle validation uses scripted transitions over `ice`, `ixgbe`, or similar drivers: repeated `napi_disable()` and `napi_enable()`, queue count changes via `ethtool -L`, interface down and up, PCI AER injected reset, devlink reload, driver unbind and bind, and unregister and re-register. Acceptance criterion: every accepted sample maps to an even live generation, and no accepted sample survives a forced odd-generation transition. Nested-NMI and hotplug validation intentionally creates heavy PEBS rates while triggering CPU online and offline churn. Acceptance criteria: no partial record is ever observed by the drain worker; nested PMI counts rise under stress but ring corruption remains zero; and late PMIs after CPU offline either drop or commit into still-live per-possible-CPU storage without use-after-free. Performance validation measures both overhead and benefit on Intel server networking stacks. Overhead targets: scheduler fast-path cost increase below noise when the static key is off, and below a low single-digit nanosecond budget on context switches involving tasks without active publication; PEBS overflow-path cost bounded by single-slot commit and validation only. Benefit targets: reduced p99 packet-processing latency and reduced CPU cycles per packet when the actuation layer steers queue affinity or budgeting based on repeated PEBS-identified memory stalls. Representative workloads include high-rate TCP and UDP on Intel Xeon with Intel Ethernet adapters, with comparisons against stock perf-only observation and against software-counter-based heuristics. Security validation ensures actuation is impossible without `CAP_NET_ADMIN` in the target namespace, is disabled for uncovered devices, is rate-limited, and rolls back cleanly. Multi-tenant tests confirm that one namespace cannot induce actuation in another and that raw PEBS-derived control decisions are suppressed when insufficient repeated evidence exists.

4. Risks & Mitigations

  • Risk: scheduler-fast-path updates could be viewed as too invasive or too expensive. Mitigation: guard the entire feature behind a static key; touch the mirror only when the feature is enabled; and perform publication republish on switch-in only for tasks with `active_net_pub != NULL`, which is a tiny subset of context switches. The core claim remains intact while minimizing steady-state overhead.
  • Risk: incomplete driver adoption could leave uncovered reset or queue-reinit paths, undermining generation correctness. Mitigation: coverage is explicit per device and per object class. If any required lifetime helper is absent, the kernel may still log PEBS observations but must disable actuation for that device, fail closed, and expose the missing hook through diagnostics.
  • Risk: PEBS rate spikes or pathological workloads could overflow the spill ring, causing sample loss. Mitigation: the design intentionally chooses drop-new over overwrite, maintains per-CPU overflow statistics, rate-limits downstream actuation so that missing some samples does not destabilize policy, and can dynamically lower PEBS sampling frequency when overflow persists.
  • Risk: PREEMPT_RT or threaded NAPI could invalidate assumptions that RX and TX spans remain continuously on-CPU. Mitigation: the novelty boundary explicitly avoids relying on full-span preemption disable. Scheduler mirroring and run-sequence checks make switch-out and migration cause sample drop unless the task is republished on switch-in, preserving correctness in threaded execution environments.
  • Risk: unusual teardown paths may still expose mixed `{id, generation}` observations if ordering is under-specified. Mitigation: IDs are immutable, generations are compared only through sidecars, odd-before-teardown and even-after-republish are mandatory ordering points, and publication acceptance requires a stable epoch snapshot plus RCU lookup of the current live generation. Mixed tuples therefore fail validation rather than being heuristically accepted.
  • Risk: PEBS-driven actuation could amplify side-channel or denial-of-service concerns. Mitigation: keep the feature off by default; require `CAP_NET_ADMIN` in the target namespace; restrict actuation to reversible controls; require repeated consistent evidence before action; impose per-device and per-netns rate limits; and expose a hard disable knob for security-sensitive deployments.
