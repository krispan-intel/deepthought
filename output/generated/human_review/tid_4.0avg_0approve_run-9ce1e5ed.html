2. Technical Invention Disclosure

2.1 Problem Statement

Linux power-management paths such as schedutil and intel_pstate primarily infer the value of higher frequency from runnable utilization, boost heuristics, and policy minima and maxima. On modern x86 systems, that misses memory-stalled phases in which additional core frequency adds little throughput but materially increases package power and thermal pressure, and it also misses address-localized cost hotspots such as remote NUMA accesses, PMEM or CXL-backed accesses, and expensive file or DAX-backed regions whose penalties are not visible in utilization alone. Existing perf-based workflows can observe these effects offline, but they do not define a bounded, lock-safe, production kernel contract that transforms x86 PEBS evidence into real-time power actuation and optional region-directed memory management without risking NMI unsafety, mmap scalability regressions, scheduler semantic breakage, cross-mm leakage, or unpredictable behavior when PEBS is unavailable, multiplexed, or contended.

2.2 Prior-Art Gap

Prior art fails at the contract boundary. Utilization-only governors do not distinguish frequency-sensitive work from backend-stalled work. perf mem, precise PEBS, and related userspace tooling can collect precise load-latency samples, but their normal operating model is AUX or ring-buffer delivery to userspace followed by offline or delayed attribution, not a kernel-fast-path actuation contract with bounded synchronization and deterministic neutral fallback. BPF cannot safely reproduce the disclosed path because the required attribution may sleep, may take mmap_read_lock, and must consult VMA metadata under bounded time budgets; BPF also does not provide a production-safe way to parse raw PEBS tuples in PMI and then feed seqcount-stable governor snapshots without verifier, helper, and context restrictions. DAMON can monitor access patterns and manage regions, but it does not teach converting sparse precise microarchitectural latency samples into bounded region-cost maps keyed by mm lifetime and VMA epochs while preserving non-overlap, hard caps, and PMU coexistence rules. The missing inventive boundary is therefore the end-to-end kernel broker: fixed-width PEBS capture in PMI, deferred no-fault VMA metadata attribution, bounded scalar and region publication, and confidence-gated actuation that preserves existing scheduler and cpufreq invariants.

2.3 Proposed Invention

The invention introduces a kernel-internal x86 PEBS broker that owns or shares a PEBS-capable precise load-latency event and a small fixed set of counting events on each CPU in a cpufreq policy. In PMI context, the broker copies only a fixed tuple of scalar fields from the PEBS record and CPU-local cached task-mm metadata into a per-CPU single-producer single-consumer ring. A sleepable worker drains the ring, resolves the mm cookie to a live per-mm context, and performs metadata-only VMA lookup under mmap_read_lock with strict nanosecond and sample budgets; samples that cannot be safely attributed are dropped or used only for policy-scalar estimation, never for address maps. The worker aggregates attributed samples into fixed-size page bins with exponential decay, bounded eviction, and deterministic merge and split rules, producing an immutable per-mm region-cost map compatible with DAMON region invariants. In parallel, it computes a per-policy bounded elasticity scalar E and confidence scalar C from weighted load-latency, sample density, multiplex ratio, attribution success, and freshness. Writers publish scalar policy snapshots under seqcount and immutable region maps under RCU. schedutil and intel_pstate readers consume only the scalar snapshot in non-sleeping paths, applying the broker only to discretionary headroom above already-computed effective minima and below existing maxima. If the PMU is unavailable, multiplexed below threshold, stale, or policy-constrained, the broker publishes confidence zero and the existing Linux behavior proceeds unchanged.
