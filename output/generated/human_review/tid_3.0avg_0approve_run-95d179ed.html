1. Executive Summary

One-Liner

An opt-in Linux kernel subsystem captures Intel PEBS load samples in the existing perf PMI flow into bounded per-CPU NMI-safe rings, revalidates each sample later with an mm_cookie plus vma_epoch contract and a no-fault page-table walk, aggregates the result into capped per-mm 2 MiB locality ledgers, and uses those ledgers only to rank already-eligible khugepaged collapse candidates and already-queued deferred-split candidates without changing THP correctness, eligibility, or reclaim semantics.
Novelty: The inventive step is not PEBS observability alone and not generic profile-guided huge pages. The novelty is a kernel-resident protocol that makes precise-address PEBS samples safe and useful for memory-management advice
Feasibility: The design is Linux-feasible because it piggybacks on the existing x86 perf/PEBS overflow path, adds only O(1) numeric copying in NMI, stores no raw task or mm pointers in interrup

2. Technical Invention Disclosure

2.1 Problem Statement

Linux Transparent Hugepage policy still relies primarily on software-visible recency and sparsity signals such as referenced and young bits, collapse-scan heuristics, and underused-page detection. Those signals reveal little about which 2 MiB windows are actually responsible for backend memory stalls, whether the expensive accesses are spread across enough 4 KiB pages to justify hugepage reach, or whether the hot accesses are confined to a tiny fragment that should instead remain splittable. The practical problem is therefore not correctness of THP itself, but poor ordering of work: khugepaged may spend scan budget collapsing windows with weak stall locality, while deferred-split may retain or process hugepages in an order that does not reflect true microarchitectural value.

2.2 Prior-Art Gap

Existing perf mem and PEBS mechanisms are observability tools, not freshness-safe in-kernel policy inputs. DAMON-style advisors observe coarse access patterns over ranges and epochs, but they do not combine precise sampled load addresses, latency weight, and data-source metadata with a proofable invalidation contract across exec, unmap, split, merge, and protection changes. Academic profile-guided hugepage work generally assumes offline or user-space profiling and does not define a kernel-safe NMI capture path, a deferred non-faulting resolver with bounded contention behavior, or a ranking-only integration that leaves all THP eligibility and reclaim semantics untouched. The gap is therefore a safe bridge from precise hardware samples to mm policy advice, not sampling itself.

2.5 Implementation Plan

Phase 1 adds CONFIG_X86_PEBS_THP_ADVISOR, the mm context fields, global cookie allocator, live-mm XArray, and vma_epoch bump sites in mm initialization, exec, exit, mprotect, munmap, mremap, and VMA adjust helpers. Phase 2 adds the per-CPU ring and perf overflow callback plumbing in the x86 Intel PEBS path, plus opt-in controls and counters. Phase 3 adds the resolver kthread, retry FIFO, no-fault walk helper, and mapping classifiers. Phase 4 adds the PMD-window ledger, lazy decay, LRU eviction, global shrinker, and memcg accounting hooks. Phase 5 adds ranking hooks to khugepaged local candidate batching and deferred-split local batch selection, guarded by a static branch so the disabled path compiles to zero-score stubs. Phase 6 adds selftests and microbenchmarks: freshness invalidation across exec and munmap, NMI ring overflow accounting, contention drop or retry behavior, disabled-path semantic equivalence, and workload studies on database, JVM, and memory-tiered benchmarks.

2.6 Validation Plan

Correctness validation has four layers. First, kernel selftests exercise cookie rollover on exec, epoch bumps on split and merge, and teardown races with forced task exit while samples are in flight. Second, stress tests force mmap_lock and PTL contention and verify bounded retry counts, drop counters, and absence of sleeping-lock warnings. Third, semantic equivalence tests compare THP behavior with the static key disabled and confirm identical collapse eligibility decisions, split eligibility decisions, sysfs policy effects, and tracepoint sequences except for the absence of advisor counters. Fourth, performance validation measures collapse success rate, TLB miss reduction, backend stall cycles, tail latency, and memory overhead on representative server workloads, with separate experiments for local DRAM-only and tiered-memory systems. The intended proof is not that every PEBS sample is preserved, but that every consumed sample is fresh, correctly attributed, and used only as ranking advice.
