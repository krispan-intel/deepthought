2. Technical Invention Disclosure

2.1 Problem Statement

High-queue-count Linux drivers for NICs, NVMe, storage HBAs, accelerators, and similar devices increasingly rely on managed MSI/MSI-X placement for throughput and latency determinism. On x86, the realized performance depends on local-APIC delivery, irq_matrix pressure, interrupt remapping, CPU hotplug, housekeeping and cpuset constraints, and driver queue resizing. Existing kernel facilities expose pieces of this problem: requested and effective affinity masks, affinity notifiers, per-CPU allocation helpers, blk-mq CPU mapping, XPS/RPS, NAPI steering, and ad hoc driver callbacks. They do not provide a unified kernel contract that simultaneously (a) derives stable, topology-ordered locality domains, (b) treats requested affinity and observed effective delivery as separate states, (c) gives pci_alloc_irq_vectors_affinity() a concrete source-of-truth for whether legacy INTx fallback is actually permitted, and (d) safely relocates only the software completion metadata whose hot cachelines should follow verified effective IRQ locality. The practical result is locality churn, stale assumptions after remapping or hotplug, unnecessary remote cacheline traffic, and driver-specific rebinding logic that cannot reliably synchronize hardirq, threaded IRQ, NAPI, and blk-mq readers.
