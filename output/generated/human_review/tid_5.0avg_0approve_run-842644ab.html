2. Technical Invention Disclosure

2.1 Problem Statement

Current Linux receive placement is assembled from separate knobs such as RSS defaults, `smp_affinity`, RPS sysfs maps, irqbalance, and driver-local affinity helpers. Those knobs treat CPUs too much like a flat pool and do not define a queue-local, kernel-generic measurement boundary for where receive work actually ran. On modern x86, the cost of moving a queue is topology dependent: local APIC and x2APIC placement, LLC sharing, SMT contention, package and NUMA distance, effective IRQ masks, managed MSI-X policy, and transient online state all matter. Existing practice therefore suffers from three kernel-level gaps: no explicit NAPI epoch contract to pair entry and exit samples, no safe CPU-attribution rule when poll execution changes CPU or enters busy-poll modes, and no lifecycle-safe commit protocol that can update RPS and IRQ state without racing hotplug, managed-vector restrictions, teardown, or external control-plane writes.

2.2 Prior-Art Gap

irqbalance periodically moves IRQs from userspace but does not observe queue-scoped NAPI epochs, does not combine IRQ and RPS with an ordered kernel commit, and does not maintain a per-queue override arbitration state. RPS sysfs updates can steer protocol processing but provide no generic in-kernel pressure bridge, no APIC-topology objective, and no managed-IRQ semantics. RFS and flow hashing optimize flow-to-CPU selection, not queue IRQ ownership or epoch attribution. XPS is transmit-side. Driver-specific affinity logic is fragmented and usually static, with no generic `napi_poll()` boundary, no migrated-epoch policy, and no claim to bounded inconsistency between RPS publication and IRQ moves. The patentable gap is therefore the concrete contract-plus-ordering-plus-arbitration mechanism, not the abstract idea of balancing load.

2.3 Proposed Invention

Introduce a per-RX-queue Queue-Epoch Pressure Bridge. Each bridge descriptor is keyed by netdevice generation, ifindex, RX queue index, NAPI identity, and dedicated IRQ number when present. On every softirq-driven invocation of the generic `napi_poll()` wrapper, the bridge records entry CPU and entry snapshot, records exit snapshot and `work_done`, validates same-CPU attribution, converts the sample into a fixed-point pressure score, and updates a hysteretic per-queue state machine. When the pressure class and dwell budget justify a move, the bridge emits a plan of type no-op, RPS-only, IRQ-only, or coordinated RPS-plus-IRQ. A deferred worker then recomputes the legal target mask as the intersection of online CPUs, bridge policy mask, queue mask, housekeeping or isolation mask when configured, IRQ effective mask, and managed mask when applicable. The worker publishes a transitional RPS map under RCU, applies IRQ affinity or affinity hint according to IRQ type, confirms the effective result, prunes or rolls back as needed, and freezes itself when an external control-plane override is detected.

2.5 Implementation Plan

Phase 1: add a bridge descriptor per RX queue keyed by ifindex, queue index, NAPI identity, IRQ number, and generation; cache x86 topology class at CPU-online time. Phase 2: instrument the generic `napi_poll()` wrapper in `net/core/dev.c` with entry and exit snapshots, same-CPU validation, fixed-point scoring, and migrated-epoch accounting. Phase 3: implement the planner and ordered worker with a per-queue mutex, managed/shared IRQ semantics, and allocate-then-publish RPS replacement under RCU. Phase 4: add notifier-driven lifecycle management for netdevice unregister, queue-count changes, and CPU hotplug; ensure teardown only bumps generation and cancels work, never forcing RTNL into the worker. Phase 5: add override arbitration with effective-affinity and RPS-map fingerprints, holdoff windows, baseline adoption, and metrics export for observability.

2.6 Validation Plan

Validate on dual-socket and single-socket Xeon systems with local APIC and x2APIC enabled, using drivers that expose dedicated MSI-X RX vectors and at least one shared-IRQ case. Measure per-queue packets per second, p99 and p99.9 receive latency, softirq CPU time, cache miss proxies, cross-socket traffic, and CPU migration rate before and after enabling the bridge. Exercise four correctness suites: (1) repoll-heavy NAPI under saturation to verify epoch pairing and no double-counting; (2) managed IRQ and shared IRQ paths to verify proper degradation and no illegal affinity writes; (3) hotplug and teardown tests to prove no stale offline CPUs remain in RPS maps and no affinity update occurs after generation change; and (4) admin-interference tests that inject `smp_affinity`, RPS sysfs, irqbalance-like moves, and RSS reconfiguration to confirm override detection, holdoff, and baseline adoption without oscillation.

4. Risks & Mitigations

  • Risk: migrated epochs reduce sample density on kernels or deployments with frequent execution-context movement. Mitigation: discard migrated epochs, require minimum valid sample density before action, and freeze the queue when migrated-sample rate exceeds 12.5 percent.
  • Risk: managed IRQ or shared IRQ semantics make hardirq movement impossible. Mitigation: degrade deterministically to RPS-only or no-op, treat `-EINVAL` and `-EPERM` as policy states, and never retry until the legal mask generation changes.
  • Risk: stale RPS maps could retain offline CPUs across hotplug. Mitigation: always revalidate under `cpus_read_lock()`, allocate maps off-path, abort on mask change, and strip offline CPUs immediately on topology-generation change.
  • Risk: oscillation against irqbalance, tuned, or direct sysfs writes. Mitigation: fingerprint observed control planes, require persistence across two samples, apply 5-second then 30-second holdoffs, and adopt a stable external baseline instead of fighting it.
  • Risk: teardown races with notifier paths or RTNL. Mitigation: keep the worker RTNL-free, use generation bumps plus work cancellation from notifier context, and make the bridge descriptor the only datapath object dereferenced after scheduling.
