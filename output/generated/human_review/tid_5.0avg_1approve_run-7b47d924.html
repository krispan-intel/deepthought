2. Technical Invention Disclosure

2.1 Problem Statement

In x86 TDX confidential VMs, most guest filesystem memory is private, but storage DMA targets memory that is explicitly shared with the host or hypervisor. That mismatch turns metadata-heavy Linux filesystem paths into conversion-dominated workloads: a 4 KiB journal descriptor or revoke block may cost more in page-state change latency, DMA setup, TLB disruption, cache pollution, and shootdown overhead than in formatting the bytes themselves. The pain is concentrated in ext4/jbd2 commit processing, where kjournald2 emits many short control-block writes under strict ordering rules, and in buffered encrypted I/O, where large folios would help throughput but plaintext must never transiently exist in shared memory. Existing Linux code has bounce pages, buffer_heads, bios, fscrypt bounce mechanisms, and general write batching, but it lacks a confidential-memory-aware lifetime object that binds shared-page ownership to a journal transaction or folio publish range, explicitly encodes which bytes may exist while shared, guarantees all shared bytes are initialized, preserves exact commit semantics, and prevents late completions from publishing or mutating objects that truncate, invalidate, or abort already made stale.

2.2 Prior-Art Gap

Prior art stops at generic staging pools, bounce buffers, encrypted I/O helpers, or journal batching. Those mechanisms do not claim or teach the confidential-memory constraint that page-state conversion must be done only on pages that are direct-mapped, DMA-capable, backend-convertible, and handled from sleepable context; they do not define a commit-epoch ownership model in which kjournald2 reserves a finite set of carrier pages spanning all descriptor, revoke, and commit blocks of one transaction; they do not specify a state machine that guarantees no uninitialized bytes are ever shared, that pages are converted before dma_map and only converted back after dma_unmap, and that all exits, including -EIO, abort, overflow, and conversion failure, end in wipe-and-reprivatize; and they do not solve the large-folio side by requiring full-range completion plus generation and mapping revalidation before publication. They also under-specify exact integration with jbd2 commit.c, the preservation of checksum timing and flush semantics, and the boundary between end_io atomic bookkeeping and any work that may sleep.
