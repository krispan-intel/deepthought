2. Technical Invention Disclosure

2.1 Problem Statement

On modern Intel servers, the interval between mutex slowpath enqueue and actual scheduler blocking is often long enough to matter but short enough that full sleep/wake overhead is wasteful. In that interval, a contended waiter may repeatedly re-read authoritative mutex state such as the lock word, owner field, and wait-list-adjacent cachelines while the current holder is about to release. On two-socket and CXL-extended systems, those reads are not cheap local polls: they trigger remote LLC lookups, UPI snoops, or CXL.cache/CXL.mem coherence activity and can elongate both unlock latency and waiter tail latency. Existing optimistic spinning helps only while the owner is running and the lock remains spin-eligible; once that path ends, Linux mostly falls back to scheduler sleep, leaving an uncovered gap where the next legitimate queue successor still needs a low-latency hint that release is imminent but must not acquire ownership through any side channel. The engineering problem is therefore to reduce remote observation during that bounded pre-sleep window without changing baseline mutex fairness, ownership, wakeup legality, signal handling, or wake_q behavior.

2.2 Prior-Art Gap

Existing Linux mutex techniques do not provide the specific combination claimed here. Optimistic spinning observes authoritative owner state directly and is not a queue-selected per-waiter notification channel. MCS-style and qspinlock-style queue nodes transfer spinning ownership semantics through private nodes, whereas Linux sleepable mutexes remain scheduler-integrated and ownership-authoritative through the mutex word and wait_lock-governed handoff. Futex waiting operates on user-visible wait addresses and does not use the existing in-kernel stack waiter object already present in the mutex slowpath. Generic MONITOR/MWAIT or WAITPKG teachings describe hardware waiting on memory locations, but not a Linux mutex design in which the existing wait_lock serializes a three-state waiter protocol that chooses between shadow notification and wake_q on the exact successor the baseline unlock path already selected. The narrowed contribution is also distinct from seqcount/RCU publication, mmu_notifier invalidation epochs, DAX mapping synchronization, and other per-range memory-management schemes: those mechanisms are outside this disclosure and are not relied upon for correctness or novelty. By centering the claims on a wait_lock-serialized advisory transport that never itself transfers ownership, the disclosure avoids the obviousness trap of merely saying use UMWAIT to optimize waiting.

2.3 Proposed Invention

For selected mutex classes on x86, extend the existing stack-resident struct mutex_waiter with: (1) a naturally aligned u32 shadow_seq monitored by the successor waiter, and (2) a u8 shadow_state with values SHADOW_OFF, SHADOW_ARMED, and SHADOW_SLEEPING. No new heap object, global table, or separate waiter allocation is introduced. Waiter-side operation: after the waiter executes the normal mutex slowpath enqueue under mutex->wait_lock and after optimistic spinning has either failed eligibility or terminated, the waiter initializes shadow_seq=0 and shadow_state=SHADOW_ARMED while still holding wait_lock. It then releases wait_lock and enters a strictly bounded advisory window. In the preferred x86 embodiment, the waiter executes UMONITOR on &waiter->shadow_seq, rechecks that shadow_state is still SHADOW_ARMED and that smp_load_acquire(shadow_seq)==0, and then executes UMWAIT or TPAUSE until a TSC deadline. The waiter never waits indefinitely: the total advisory budget is selected from a small policy table with defaults of 2 microseconds for ordinary remote-NUMA mutex classes and 6 microseconds for mutex classes explicitly annotated as CXL-latency-sensitive, with a hard cap of 8 microseconds and at most four wait slices. After each wake or slice expiration the waiter checks need_resched(), signal_pending_state() for interruptible or killable lock acquisitions, preempt_count()==0, interrupts-enabled state, and CPU or guest policy eligibility. If any disqualifier appears, or if the deadline expires, the waiter reacquires mutex->wait_lock and either (a) observes that shadow_seq changed or the real mutex became acquirable and therefore does not sleep, or (b) atomically changes shadow_state from SHADOW_ARMED to SHADOW_SLEEPING under wait_lock and falls through to the baseline scheduler sleep path. Unlock-side operation: unlock remains authoritative and queue-faithful. While holding mutex->wait_lock, unlock selects the same next waiter the baseline mutex code would select. Still under that same lock, unlock performs baseline owner and handoff bookkeeping and then inspects only that chosen waiter's shadow_state. If shadow_state equals SHADOW_ARMED, unlock performs a release-ordered increment of next->shadow_seq and does not queue a wake_q wake for that waiter in that critical section. If shadow_state equals SHADOW_SLEEPING, unlock performs the ordinary wake_q_add() path. Because both the waiter transition from SHADOW_ARMED to SHADOW_SLEEPING and the unlock-side decision are serialized by wait_lock, unlock cannot race a waiter into a state where both the shadow notification and wake_q wake are missed. The shadow_seq is advisory only: after observing a changed shadow_seq, the waiter must still re-read the actual mutex state with acquire semantics and acquire ownership only through the real mutex algorithm. If acquisition fails, the waiter continues through the unchanged slowpath. Lifetime and stack-safety rule: the monitored address is valid because the waiter object already remains live on the task's kernel stack for the duration of mutex slowpath participation. The waiter clears or changes shadow_state only under wait_lock and only before unlinking its list node or returning from slowpath. Unlock dereferences a successor waiter only while holding wait_lock and only through the live wait_list linkage, so it cannot legally retain the stack pointer after the waiter is removed. This makes a stack-resident monitor target safe without introducing refcounts or separate storage. Capability and virtualization gating: the advisory window is entered only when current executes in sleepable process context, interrupts are enabled, preempt_count()==0, need_resched() is false, the mutex acquisition mode permits blocking, and policy marks the CPU as eligible. The WAITPKG embodiment additionally requires CPUID enumeration of WAITPKG, a kernel static key such as mutex_shadow_wait_enabled, a boot or runtime policy knob such as mutex_shadow_wait=off or on, and an x86 policy routine that programs MSR_IA32_UMWAIT_CONTROL to a capped shallow state and maximum time budget. The feature defaults to disabled in guests unless the hypervisor explicitly exposes WAITPKG and kernel policy marks the instruction as non-trapping and acceptable; otherwise the waiter skips directly to the standard sleep path. MONITOR/MWAIT can be supported only as an optional, separately gated embodiment and is disabled by default. Any failure of CPUID, MSR setup, firmware permission, or virtualization safety does not alter correctness; it simply bypasses the advisory phase.

Ordering and correctness contract:
1. wait_lock serialization: the enqueue plus shadow_state=SHADOW_ARMED publication happens-before unlock's inspection of that waiter because both occur in mutex->wait_lock critical sections. Likewise, the waiter's SHADOW_ARMED to SHADOW_SLEEPING transition happens-before any unlock that subsequently inspects the state under the same lock.
2. Shadow notification ordering: unlock performs the shadow_seq increment with release ordering; after UMWAIT returns, the waiter observes shadow_seq with acquire ordering before re-reading the real mutex state. On x86 TSO the hardware ordering is already strong, but release/acquire primitives are still used so the compiler cannot move accesses around the monitor-wait sequence and so the Linux Kernel Memory Model contract is explicit. UMONITOR and UMWAIT are treated as wait primitives, not ordering primitives.
3. Real ownership ordering: after seeing shadow_seq advance, the waiter still acquires the mutex only through the normal mutex algorithm using the same acquire semantics as baseline trylock or handoff logic. Therefore shadow_seq visibility cannot manufacture ownership or bypass fairness.
4. wake_q interaction: unlock chooses exactly one notification path for the chosen successor while holding wait_lock. If the waiter is SHADOW_ARMED, the waiter remains runnable and must not also be placed on wake_q in that state snapshot. If the waiter has already changed to SHADOW_SLEEPING, unlock must use wake_q_add(). Because the waiter cannot change state without wait_lock, no execution can miss both notifications or sleep indefinitely due to reordering between shadow_seq publication and wake_q batching.
5. Stack validity: unlock can reach waiter->shadow_seq only from the linked wait_list while holding wait_lock; the waiter cannot unlink and return until it first clears or transitions shadow_state under the same lock. This excludes use-after-return on the stack monitor address.

2.5 Implementation Plan

1. Extend struct mutex_waiter with u32 shadow_seq and u8 shadow_state, keep shadow_seq naturally aligned, and add tracepoints or counters for shadow_arm, shadow_poke, shadow_timeout, shadow_fallback_reason, and shadow_successful_acquire. 2. Add a static key and sysctl or boot knob for the feature, plus a small per-mutex-class or lockdep-class policy table that chooses advisory budgets of 0, 2, or 6 microseconds with an absolute cap of 8 microseconds. Budgets are selected only for mutexes whose hold times and topology justify the optimization. 3. Implement mutex_shadow_wait_allowed() so the advisory path executes only in sleepable process context with IRQs enabled, preempt_count()==0, need_resched() clear, no disallowed signal pending for the acquisition mode, and no real-time or policy constraints that require immediate scheduler handoff. 4. Implement x86 capability gating: require WAITPKG CPUID support for the preferred embodiment, configure MSR_IA32_UMWAIT_CONTROL to a shallow allowed state and bounded timeout, default the feature off under virtualization unless the guest explicitly exposes safe WAITPKG behavior, and treat every failure as a clean fallback to baseline mutex sleep. Keep MONITOR/MWAIT behind a separate disabled-by-default knob. 5. Modify the waiter slowpath after optimistic spinning ends so enqueue plus SHADOW_ARMED publication occurs under wait_lock, followed by a bounded UMONITOR and UMWAIT or TPAUSE loop that is sliced into at most four sub-waits, each followed by need_resched(), signal, and policy checks and a real mutex reacquire attempt. 6. Modify unlock only inside the existing wait_lock critical section: select the baseline successor, perform normal owner and handoff bookkeeping, and then choose between a release-ordered shadow_seq increment or the baseline wake_q_add() depending on the successor's serialized shadow_state. 7. On timeout or disqualification, reacquire wait_lock and transition SHADOW_ARMED to SHADOW_SLEEPING before entering the unchanged scheduler-block path, so wake_q remains the only wake mechanism once the waiter is truly asleep. 8. Keep the advisory transport out of the independent claim scope of ownership transfer: claims and implementation must show that shadow_seq never grants the lock, never reorders the queue, never scans for alternative successors, and never suppresses fallback sleep if acquisition does not promptly succeed.

4. Risks & Mitigations

  • Risk: WAITPKG or MONITOR/MWAIT behavior may be trapped, disabled, or power-policy-sensitive on some platforms or hypervisors. Mitigation: make hardware waiting optional, default-off in guests, guarded by CPUID, MSR, and policy checks and a static key, with immediate fallback to the unchanged scheduler path.
  • Risk: a poorly bounded advisory wait could delay higher-priority work or conflict with scheduler expectations. Mitigation: permit the path only in sleepable process context with IRQs enabled and preemption allowed, cap the window to a small fixed budget, slice long waits, and check need_resched() and signal state between slices.
  • Risk: lost wakeups between shadow notification and wake_q could create indefinite sleep. Mitigation: serialize the waiter SHADOW_ARMED to SHADOW_SLEEPING transition and the unlock-side notification choice with the existing mutex wait_lock, and validate the contract with LKMM litmus tests and injected race tests.
  • Risk: stack-resident monitor targets could be accused of unsafe lifetime. Mitigation: tie dereference legality strictly to wait-list linkage under wait_lock and require state clearing or transition before waiter unlink or return, so unlock never retains the pointer after list removal.
  • Risk: prior art could characterize the work as merely using UMWAIT for mutex waiting. Mitigation: center the independent claims on queue-faithful successor selection, wait_lock-serialized state transitions, and advisory-only notification, while leaving WAITPKG as a narrower dependent embodiment.
  • Risk: benefits may be workload-specific and absent on low-latency systems. Mitigation: use opt-in mutex classes, topology-aware budgets, tracepoint-driven tuning, and runtime disablement when counters show poor win rate or excessive timeout fallback.
