2. Technical Invention Disclosure

2.1 Problem Statement

Linux journaling filesystems running on x86 servers with CXL.mem face two distinct but interacting inefficiencies. First, fsync and related durability paths remain bound to the home journal device even when the platform presents a much lower-latency CXL-attached persistent aperture. Second, buffered page cache can occupy scarce local DRAM long after specific file-offset regions have become predictably cold, while hotter regions of the same files and other workloads compete for the near tier. Existing Linux mechanisms do not provide a kernel-correct way to acknowledge an earlier durability point from a CXL region that is actually replay-authoritative, and they do not provide a kernel-correct way to demote file-cache folios by file-offset heat without per-folio tags, without long-held inode locks, and without races against truncate, hole-punch, remap/reflink, invalidate, inode eviction, or concurrent faults and readahead. A defensible solution therefore needs two explicit contracts. First, early commit completion must be tied to a registered aperture whose persistence domain, flush method, exclusivity, namespace identity, poison/error reporting, and recovery role are attested by the kernel, and it must define the exact sealed record format, replay ordering, revoke handling, and revocation/drain behavior if the aperture faults after APERTURE_DURABLE but before HOME_DURABLE. Second, range-based page-cache tiering must define the exact mapping mutations that invalidate a range intent, the exact XArray walk and folio revalidation sequence used before migration, the exact demotion eligibility predicate, the stable node-selection rule under changing memory-tier topology, and the precedence and backoff rules relative to reclaim, MGLRU, workingset refaults, memcg, cpuset hardwalls, and x86/CXL bandwidth pressure. The intended scope is regular block-backed page cache on journaling filesystems, not DAX, not shmem/tmpfs, and not swapcache.

2.3 Proposed Invention

The invention has two coordinated but independently deployable planes. 1. Journal plane - recovery-qualified CXL aperture. The initial embodiment targets ext4 plus jbd2, although the same contract applies to other jbd2-based filesystems. At mount time the journal may register a bounded CXL.mem aperture only if the kernel can attest all of the following: the aperture belongs to a persistent or persistence-qualified CXL region with stable namespace identity, the platform exposes a valid cache flush path for that region, poison and hot-remove are surfaced to the kernel, the aperture is reserved exclusively for this journal instance, and the filesystem superblock records that this aperture is a recovery-authoritative replay source. The aperture contains an A/B anchor area and a ring of transaction slots. In jbd2 commit processing, after the transaction is closed and revoke information is finalized but before fsync waiters are released, the journal serializes the exact transaction byte stream into the next aperture slot. The slot record is not a semantic summary; it is a sealed replay image containing: a header with magic, filesystem UUID or journal UUID, mount generation, transaction tid, payload length, home-log sequence metadata, and checksum; the payload bytes for the descriptor blocks, logged metadata blocks, revoke blocks, and commit block in the same replay order jbd2 would normally issue; and a footer that repeats tid, payload checksum, and slot state. Slot states advance monotonically as PREPARING, SEALED, COPYING_HOME, HOME_DURABLE, or REVOKED. APERTURE_DURABLE is granted only after the slot payload and footer are persisted and the A/B anchor is atomically advanced to the highest contiguous SEALED tid. HOME_DURABLE is granted later, after the ordinary home-journal commit is durable on the home device. Recovery uses the aperture as a first replay source. On mount after crash, the kernel scans the A/B anchor copies, chooses the newest valid generation, and replays only those slots whose header and footer agree, whose checksum validates, and whose state is SEALED or COPYING_HOME, in strictly increasing tid order. Because the payload includes the original revoke blocks, revoke semantics are identical to home-journal replay rather than reconstructed after the fact. After aperture replay reaches the last valid tid, normal home-journal replay proceeds as usual. If the registered aperture reports poison, hot-remove, health downgrade, namespace withdrawal, or any event that invalidates its recovery authority, the journal enters a mandatory revocation-and-drain state machine. New transactions may still commit, but they can no longer complete at APERTURE_DURABLE; they must wait for HOME_DURABLE. Outstanding SEALED but not HOME_DURABLE slots are processed in tid order. If the slots remain readable, the journal synchronously copies them to the home journal, marks them HOME_DURABLE, advances the drain pointer, and then retires the aperture. If any outstanding slot is unreadable or its seal validation fails, the journal aborts and the filesystem transitions to its configured fail-stop policy such as remount-read-only or panic; there is no silent success-shaped fallback. This is what makes the early completion contract kernel-correct rather than a cache promise. 2. Tiering plane - mapping-scoped range intents without per-folio tags. Each participating address_space owns a sidecar heat structure keyed by file-offset buckets, not by folios. Bucket identity is fixed by logical file offset, for example index >> bucket_shift, so truncate does not shift bucket numbering. Each bucket stores at least an atomic heat counter, last-cold-generation, cooldown-until timestamp, and recent-failure count. The sidecar also contains an invalidation seqcount and a refcount-plus-RCU lifetime rule so workers can safely outlive concurrent inode operations without dereferencing freed heat state. Heat accounting occurs only at buffered I/O hook points that already know the mapping and index: when buffered reads return folios from the page cache, when readahead installs uptodate folios for the mapping, and when buffered write_end dirties a range. Bucket increments are atomic; the design does not require fictional per-folio tags. File-size change does not renumber buckets. On truncate or punch-out beyond new i_size, the kernel bumps the mapping invalidation seqcount, updates the sidecar size generation, and workers simply ignore or later reclaim buckets wholly beyond i_size. On inode eviction the sidecar is detached and freed by RCU after outstanding worker references drain, so heat updates never target freed storage. 3. Exact invalidation seqcount contract. The invalidation seqcount protects one thing only: whether a previously selected file-offset range still names the same valid page-cache region of the same mapping. It is bumped under the mapping's invalidation serialization, i.e. while holding filemap_invalidate_lock exclusively and whatever filesystem lock that operation already requires, for mapping-altering operations such as truncate, hole-punch, collapse-range, insert-range, remap or reflink or dedupe operations that change which logical offsets are backed by which folios, invalidate_inode_pages2 or range invalidation that removes page-cache folios, whole-inode eviction or teardown, and any filesystem-specific operation that deletes or replaces folios in the mapping. The seqcount is not bumped for ordinary dirtying, write_begin or write_end, readahead, or mmap teardown, because those do not change logical-offset-to-folio identity. Mmap teardown instead changes mapcount, and mapped folios are filtered out during final revalidation. Shmem and swapcache are outside the scope of this embodiment. 4. Bounded XArray enumeration. A demotion worker acts only on cold buckets that have remained below threshold for at least two aging intervals and are not in cooldown. For a selected bucket range it snapshots seq = read_seqcount_begin(inv_seq), then walks mapping->i_pages using the XArray under RCU, taking speculative folio references with folio_try_get_rcu. The walk is bounded - for example by number of folios, bytes, and wall-clock budget per pass - so the worker never holds a long lock over a large file. XA retry entries, shadow entries, and concurrent deletions are skipped naturally. After each batch the worker resumes from the last scanned index. If concurrent faults or insertions keep repopulating the range, the worker does not spin forever: repeated seqcount mismatches or repeated revalidation failures clear the current intent, increase the bucket failure count, and push the bucket into cooldown before it is reconsidered. 5. Exact per-folio eligibility and revalidation sequence. The worker never migrates based on bucket coldness alone. For each speculative candidate it performs the following sequence: acquire filemap_invalidate_lock_shared for the mapping; if read_seqcount_retry shows the invalidation seqcount changed, drop the reference, release the shared invalidate lock, clear or age the intent, and skip; otherwise try to lock the folio; once the folio is locked, re-check that folio->mapping is still the same mapping, the folio index is still inside the intended bucket range, the invalidation seqcount still matches, the folio is uptodate, clean, not under writeback, not mapped into any process page tables, not DMA-pinned or FOLL_PIN-pinned, not mlocked or unevictable, not swapcache, not a huge or otherwise non-migratable compound folio unless the filesystem and migration helper explicitly support it, and not already isolated for reclaim or migration. The predicate does not rely on mapped_to_disk-style heuristics; correctness comes from same-mapping plus same-index plus clean plus writeback-free plus unmapped plus migratable state. Only after those checks pass does the worker isolate the folio from the LRU while still holding the folio lock. After isolation it can drop filemap_invalidate_lock_shared and invoke the standard migration helper toward the chosen demotion node, letting filesystem-specific migration flow through the normal migrate_folio path where required. If any check fails, or if lock, isolation, or migrate_folio fails, the action is exact and conservative: unlock if locked, drop the folio reference, skip migration, clear or age the range intent as appropriate, and increment backoff so the worker does not livelock on the same hot or pinned folio. No ambiguous folio is migrated. This lock ordering is deliberate. Writers such as truncate and hole-punch already serialize invalidation by taking filemap_invalidate_lock exclusively before they destroy or replace folios. The worker takes filemap_invalidate_lock_shared before final validation, then folio lock, then LRU isolation. It does not hold i_rwsem across migration, and it does not need mmap_lock because mapped folios are ineligible. That avoids AB/BA deadlocks while still ensuring that a mapping-altering writer either happens entirely before the worker snapshot or forces a seqcount mismatch or mapping mismatch during revalidation. 6. Target-node selection, memcg correctness, and topology changes. Target selection is not a private heuristic. The worker asks the kernel's existing memory-tier demotion topology for the next lower-tier node from the folio's current NUMA node and refuses migration if there is no current demotion target, if the target node is offline, if the demotion graph changed mid-operation, if the target is outside the allowed cpuset hardwall, or if the filesystem mount disallows that node class. Page-cache placement is not reconstructed from any individual task's VMA mempolicy; instead the invention follows the same policy domain that reclaim-driven file-cache demotion already uses: online nodes, demotion graph, cpuset hardwalls, and standard memcg charge migration. The worker does not implement custom charge transfers. It relies on the ordinary folio migration machinery so memcg charging and per-node statistics remain consistent. If the normal migration helper refuses the move, the folio stays put and the worker backs off. 7. Interaction with reclaim, MGLRU, workingset, and NUMA balancing. The invention does not replace reclaim and does not fight it. When direct reclaim, memcg reclaim, or kswapd pressure is active beyond a configured threshold, proactive demotion pauses and reclaim owns the demotion path. MGLRU and workingset still decide eviction and refault behavior globally. The invention yields a different outcome because it acts on file-offset locality inside a mapping before those folios necessarily become the oldest pages in the entire machine. That allows the kernel to free near-tier DRAM from cold regions of large files while leaving hotter regions of the same files in DRAM. To avoid demote-refault-ping-pong, a bucket must remain cold for multiple aging intervals before demotion, and any refault, buffered read hit, or write_end activity immediately reheats the bucket and starts a cooldown interval during which the worker will not re-demote that range. Promotion remains implicit through ordinary refault and readahead allocation; the invention never launches a competing proactive promotion engine. NUMA balancing is not modified because it does not directly manage unmapped file-cache folio placement in this path. 8. x86 and CXL hardware-software governor. A per-socket or per-memory-domain governor samples only boot-discovered x86-visible counters already exposed through the normal perf or uncore monitoring paths: integrated memory controller read and write bandwidth, interconnect or CXL fabric occupancy proxy, and LLC miss pressure or offcore miss rate. Those signals never trigger ad hoc control in atomic paths and never renegotiate immutable CPU state. They only clamp three bounded knobs: the maximum number of bytes or transactions allowed to remain APERTURE_DURABLE but not yet HOME_DURABLE, the maximum proactive demotion bytes per scan interval, and the readahead ceiling for buckets already classified cold. If home-journal queue depth rises, the governor shrinks the APERTURE_DURABLE window so deferred home copy cannot run away. If CXL fabric occupancy and LLC miss pressure are high, it shrinks demotion bandwidth and cold-range readahead. If near-tier pressure is high but CXL occupancy is low, it allows larger demotion batches. If the platform lacks the relevant uncore counters, the governor falls back to conservative static caps and the correctness contract is unchanged. 9. Why the combination is new. The journal plane contributes a revocable recovery-authoritative aperture contract with exact sealed transaction images, dual durable anchors, and mandatory drain or abort. The tiering plane contributes mapping-scoped invalidation fencing that allows range intents without per-folio metadata, plus bounded XArray walking, exact folio revalidation, demotion-graph-constrained target selection, and reclaim-aware backoff. The coordination matters: both planes are mount-local, both can be throttled by the same x86 and CXL domain governor, and neither requires fictional Linux primitives. The result is a filesystem optimization architecture that improves commit latency and page-cache residency while staying inside real Linux kernel locking, recovery, and migration rules.
