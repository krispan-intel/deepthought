2. Technical Invention Disclosure

2.1 Problem Statement

Virtualized AMX workloads such as BF16 and INT8 GEMM, tiled convolution lowering, recommendation inference, database vector scans, and matrix-factorization phases often execute in short compute epochs whose guest-physical working sets are stable at 2 MiB granularity for only a few milliseconds. Current KVM TDP MMU hugepage promotion is host-reactive: it derives PMD suitability from observed split, fault, and access history after the fact. The guest, however, knows before the hot phase begins which aligned GPA extents will be touched repeatedly. Under dirty logging, access tracking, memslot updates, nested virtualization, or repeated PMD split and re-promote cycles, KVM may miss that stable window and leave AMX kernels executing on fragmented 4 KiB SPTEs, paying avoidable nested page-walk cost, SPTE churn, TLB pressure, and refault overhead. The technical problem is therefore to expose short-lived guest phase knowledge to KVM without allowing the guest to force illegal hugepages, without introducing attacker-controlled lookup structures or unbounded host work, and without creating correctness holes during invalidation, memslot replacement, migration, or confidential-guest modes.

2.2 Prior-Art Gap

Existing host-side KVM heuristics decide second-level hugepage promotion from host-observed access history, not from a VMM-controlled guest signal tied to short AMX compute epochs. Guest THP, MADV_HUGEPAGE, and in-guest hugepage allocators affect only first-level mappings and do not securely express second-level intent to KVM. Userspace choices such as hugetlb-backed memory, THP-backed host memory, ballooning, Hyper-V or VMware enlightenments, or virtio memory hints either change backing indirectly or communicate different classes of memory-management intent; they do not define a secure, versioned, per-vCPU transport with explicit ring semantics, token and generation rebinding, per-memslot cookie binding, O(1) direct-index chunk metadata, explicit invalidation-coupled clearing, bounded TTL semantics, and a bias-only hook after all baseline KVM hugepage disallow predicates have already passed. No known mechanism combines VMM mediation, per-vCPU pinned transport attestation, memslot cookie replacement on KVM_SET_USER_MEMORY_REGION changes, quantitative rate and footprint limits, and a post-eligibility PMD bonus that never forces mapping changes and never schedules background promotion scans.

2.3 Proposed Invention

Introduce an optional KVM feature named AMX Extent Hints. A cooperative guest runtime or paravirtual driver emits a descriptor at AMX phase boundaries, typically when switching to a new tiled GEMM, convolution, matrix-factorization, or analytical-vector epoch. The descriptor specifies a 2 MiB-aligned GPA start, a bounded chunk count, a bounded TTL, an epoch identifier, and a reuse class. The VMM must explicitly enable the feature at the VM level, mark eligible memslots, bind one transport ring per vCPU, and provision a per-vCPU token and channel generation. KVM drains descriptors only from the bound channel of the current vCPU, validates alignment, range, quotas, token, generation, and current memslot identity, and then stores accepted state in a preallocated per-memslot direct-index array keyed by memslot-relative 2 MiB chunk index. During TDP MMU large-page evaluation, after the normal code path has already determined that a PMD leaf is legally permissible, KVM may add a single constant score bonus if a live, non-expired, non-suppressed hint exists for the exact chunk. Hints never override dirty logging, NX hugepage restrictions, access tracking, max-page-level checks, mmu_notifier invalidation, nested virtualization policy, or protected-memory restrictions; they only help choose PMD where PMD is already allowed. Hints are ephemeral, non-migrated, automatically expired on wall-clock time, ignored during deschedule or guest stall once expired, and cleared or suppressed in O(1) or range-bounded time on invalidation events.
