2. Technical Invention Disclosure

2.1 Problem Statement

Linux IRQ placement is usually driven by topology, queue count, affinity hints, and coarse interrupt rate rather than by the actual microarchitectural cost of servicing a given device interrupt on a given CPU. Two interrupts with similar rates can impose very different cost because one burns backend memory-stall cycles, trips remote-cache or NUMA penalties, or perturbs branch-history and LLC state while another is mostly cache-hot. Existing observability can sample those effects, but it does not provide an authoritative, NMI-safe identity that says a PEBS record belongs to a currently executing external-device hardirq leaf handler. Sampling by IP, vector, or task is insufficient because generic interrupt code is shared, vectors may be remapped, hierarchical domains hide physical provenance behind parent domains, and shared, threaded, chained, per-CPU, IPI, and wake-thread-only paths do not define a single controllable leaf handler interval. Even when evidence exists, Linux today lacks a closed-loop controller that converts such evidence into affinity changes without violating managed affinity, administrator pinning, nohz_full or isolcpus housekeeping, hotplug races, and irqchip or interrupt-remapping correctness.

2.2 Prior-Art Gap

Prior art falls into four buckets and leaves a specific gap. First, genericirq, irqbalance, managed affinity, and driver-supplied affinity hints can move interrupts, but they use load or topology heuristics and are blind to PEBS-visible service cost. Second, perf, PEBS, load-latency profiling, and LBR tracing can measure kernel activity, but they attribute to code locations or contexts rather than to a validated IRQ service interval, so they cannot safely drive IRQ routing under vector aliasing, remapping, shared generic code, or nested interrupts. Third, tracepoint-based or ftrace-based IRQ attribution starts too late, runs in instrumentation paths rather than the PMU drain path, and cannot provide an NMI-safe per-dispatch identity snapshot that survives sampled-IP ambiguity. Fourth, existing in-kernel balancing heuristics do not define a generation-safe identity scheme that prevents stale samples from being applied after request_irq, free_irq, shared-action mutation, or irq-domain teardown. The gap is therefore a precise bridge between x86 PEBS evidence and Linux IRQ actuation: a non-pointer, generation-tagged, per-dispatch IRQ identity published at the generic dispatch boundary, read by PMI/NMI without descriptor chasing, and consumed later by a constrained controller that never bypasses genericirq or irqchip policy.

2.3 Proposed Invention

The invention adds three cooperating kernel components. 1) Dispatch-gated identity publication. In the generic IRQ event path, after vector-to-desc resolution and after the descriptor has been classified through desc, irq_data, and the action chain, the kernel executes a new enter/exit pair around the sole eligible primary hardirq action. The hook point is immediately before invoking that action and immediately after it returns, i.e. after unusual irqchip and hierarchical-domain flows have already converged into the generic desc path and cannot bypass classification by presenting only raw vectors. Eligibility is deterministic: desc->action must exist; exactly one action must be present; the action must have a real primary hardirq handler; the action must not be threaded-only or wake-thread-only; the interrupt must not be per-CPU, IPI, NMI, chained-parent, nested-thread, or otherwise non-migratable; and actuation additionally requires that the IRQ can legally change affinity through existing core helpers. Shared descriptors with more than one action are explicitly not treated as a single leaf handler and are excluded from control to avoid mixed-device ambiguity. 2) Concrete active-interrupt cookie ABI. Each CPU owns a seqcount-protected active state: { u32 virq; u32 config_gen; u16 depth; u8 flags; u8 stack_overflow; u32 cpu; u64 enter_tsc; }. No desc pointer, action pointer, or hwirq-domain pointer is published to PMI/NMI readers. virq identifies the current Linux IRQ instance, config_gen identifies the current configuration of that virq, depth identifies nesting level within a bounded per-CPU stack, flags identify eligible versus observe-disabled state, cpu binds the cookie to the publishing CPU, and enter_tsc bounds the service interval. The per-CPU state is a bounded stack, not a single overwrite slot, so higher-priority external interrupts that nest on top of a running IRQ push a new frame instead of destroying the underlying attribution context. A depth of four or eight entries is sufficient for practical external-IRQ nesting; if the stack limit is exceeded the writer sets stack_overflow and PMIs on that CPU drop attribution until unwind clears the condition. The writer uses write_seqcount_begin/end around depth and slot updates. The PEBS PMI/NMI reader uses READ_ONCE plus seqcount retry to read depth and the top frame atomically; it accepts a sample only if the seqcount is stable, the live flag is set, stack_overflow is clear, and cookie.cpu matches the local CPU. This proves the sample occurred inside the intended validated leaf-handler interval without chasing irq_desc or sleeping. 3) Generation-safe identity and deferred control. Because virq numbers can be recycled and action chains can change, each irq instance has a sidecar config_gen that is incremented on every successful topology or eligibility-affecting mutation: __setup_irq action insertion, __free_irq action removal, transitions between single-action and shared-action states, affinity-management reconfiguration, and irq-domain deactivation or free that can later recycle the virq. PEBS samples are stored under the key <virq, config_gen, cpu>. During aggregation or before actuation, the controller looks up the current sidecar and drops any evidence whose config_gen no longer matches, preventing use-after-free, recycled-virq, and stale-sharedness errors. The PEBS data path is bounded and NMI-safe. On supported x86 CPUs, a kernel-owned PEBS event group records fixed service-cost features such as retired-load latency above threshold, sampled data-source class, and cycle cost; when LBR-assisted PEBS is available, the handler also folds the captured LBR sequence into a small service-shape signature so the controller can distinguish cache-hot fast paths from memory-stalled slow paths without exporting raw branches. The PMI path never allocates, never grabs sleeping locks, and never touches irq_desc. It appends a compact fixed-size record into a per-CPU power-of-two ring, for example 2048 entries, using only local atomic head reservation; if the ring is full it drops the new record and increments a lost counter. A periodic process-context worker drains the ring, aggregates per-IRQ per-CPU EWMA cost metrics, and feeds a constrained controller. The controller computes candidate affinity masks only as subsets of an allowed envelope. For managed IRQs, the envelope is the current managed effective mask intersected with housekeeping_cpumask(HK_TYPE_MANAGED_IRQ) and cpu_online_mask. For non-managed IRQs, the envelope is a controller-maintained administrator cap mask that is initialized from the current effective affinity when control is enabled and refreshed whenever userspace, irqbalance, or a driver changes affinity outside the controller. The controller further intersects with cpu_online_mask and never widens beyond the latest administrator or core-provided envelope. Actuation uses ordinary irq_set_affinity; thus irqchip callbacks, vector allocation, Intel interrupt-remapping IRTE programming, and queued invalidation remain owned by the existing core. If the core rejects or truncates the requested mask, the controller records the returned mask or error, backs off, and treats that result as a policy veto rather than retrying aggressively. Hysteresis makes the loop stable: the controller proposes a move only after an IRQ shows a configurable cost delta, for example at least 15 percent better on another allowed CPU across three consecutive aggregation windows, and only after a minimum dwell interval, for example 500 ms, since the last successful move. External affinity changes by userspace, a driver, or irqbalance reset the controller baseline by bumping the observed affinity generation; under races, external writers win because the controller revalidates config_gen and affinity generation before applying and abandons any stale plan.

2.4 Architecture

Control-flow overview:

CPU receives external interrupt
  -> arch entry / vector delivery
  -> vector or remapped source resolves to irq_desc
  -> desc->handle_irq(...) generic flow
  -> handle_irq_event_percpu(desc)
       -> classify desc + action chain + irq_data
       -> if single-action validated external-device hardirq:
            write_seqcount_begin(per_cpu_active_irq.seq)
            push {virq, config_gen, depth, flags, cpu, enter_tsc}
            write_seqcount_end(...)
            call primary hardirq action
            write_seqcount_begin(...)
            pop active frame / clear live bit
            write_seqcount_end(...)
          else:
            call action path with no published cookie

Concurrent PEBS PMI/NMI on same CPU
  -> read per_cpu_active_irq with seqcount retry
  -> if live and stable and no overflow:
       append compact sample to per-CPU PEBS ring keyed by <virq, config_gen, cpu>
     else:
       drop sample

Periodic worker (process context)
  -> drain per-CPU rings
  -> aggregate EWMA service-cost per <virq, cpu, config_gen>
  -> revalidate current sidecar generation and allowed masks
  -> intersect desired mask with managed/admin cap, housekeeping, online CPUs
  -> apply via irq_set_affinity(virq, mask)
  -> irq core / irqchip / Intel IOMMU perform normal vector or IRTE updates

Concrete kernel placement and serialization:
- Dispatch publication occurs in the generic IRQ event path immediately around the sole primary hardirq action, after desc resolution and after eligibility checks see the final desc, irq_data, and action-chain state.
- Eligibility metadata is cached in a per-IRQ sidecar published under seqcount or RCU-style replacement, but the authoritative mutations occur under the same serialization already used by IRQ core setup and teardown: action-chain changes under request path locking and desc lock, and affinity changes under core affinity locking.
- The hot path performs only per-CPU writes, so there is no cross-CPU cacheline bouncing.
- Nested external IRQs use a per-CPU stack; NMI/PMI reads only the current top frame.
- Chained parent interrupts, per-CPU interrupts, IPIs, NMIs, timers, and wake-thread-only paths never publish cookies and therefore cannot be misattributed.

2.5 Implementation Plan

1. Add a per-IRQ sidecar keyed by virq that stores config_gen, eligibility bits, action-count classification, current allowed-envelope state, and last-controller metadata. config_gen is incremented on every successful __setup_irq or __free_irq mutation that changes the action chain or leaf eligibility, and on irq-domain deactivation or free that can release or recycle the virq. For shared IRQs, any transition into or out of action_count != 1 increments config_gen so stale evidence cannot survive a sharing change. 2. Implement the eligibility classifier using only concrete IRQ-core state. Control-eligible means: exactly one action; action->handler is a real primary handler rather than the wake-thread-only default primary handler; no threaded-only semantics; not per-CPU according to irqd_is_per_cpu(&desc->irq_data); not an NMI or IPI descriptor; not a chained-parent descriptor lacking a normal action chain; and affinity-actuable according to the existing core predicate used for user affinity changes, e.g. irq_can_set_affinity_usr(virq), plus at least two CPUs in the current allowed envelope. Mixed shared actions are marked control-ineligible. Optionally, primary-plus-threaded handlers can be configured as observe-only, but the preferred embodiment excludes them from control so the evidence represents only hardirq service, not thread context. 3. Hook the generic IRQ event path immediately before and after invocation of the sole eligible primary hardirq handler. The enter helper pushes a per-CPU frame into a bounded stack and publishes it with seqcount; the exit helper pops it. The writer stores virq, config_gen, current CPU, nesting depth, entry TSC, and eligibility flags only. No desc pointer or action pointer is copied into the active ABI, removing UAF risk from the PMI path. 4. Extend the x86 perf or PMU integration with a kernel-owned PEBS event set restricted to kernel collection. The PMI path reads the active frame using seqcount retry and appends a fixed-size record into a preallocated per-CPU ring. The record contains <virq, config_gen, cpu, delta_tsc or sample timestamp, selected PEBS latency fields, optional LBR-derived service-shape hash, and status bits>. The PMI path uses only preallocated per-CPU memory, local atomic index reservation, and drop counters on overflow. A per-CPU recursion flag guards against pathological reentry while draining PEBS; if reentry is detected the nested sample is dropped and counted. 5. Create a periodic worker that drains the rings every configurable interval, for example 20 to 50 ms, and maintains per-IRQ per-CPU EWMAs for total service cost, memory-stall intensity, and confidence. The worker discards any sample whose <virq, config_gen> no longer matches the current sidecar. This is the anti-UAF and anti-reuse rule: evidence is valid only if the irq instance still exists and its current config_gen exactly matches the sample's config_gen. 6. Build the allowed affinity envelope before every actuation. For managed IRQs, start from the core-managed mask and intersect with housekeeping_cpumask(HK_TYPE_MANAGED_IRQ) and cpu_online_mask. For non-managed IRQs, start from the controller's administrator cap mask, which is initialized from the current effective affinity when control is enabled and refreshed on every externally observed affinity change. The controller never expands beyond that envelope. If a CPU hotplug event removes a CPU, the hotplug notifier invalidates pending plans, masks out offline CPUs, and resets dwell timers. 7. Serialize controller decisions with a per-IRQ controller mutex and generation revalidation. The worker snapshots config_gen, affinity generation, and current effective affinity; computes a candidate; rechecks those generations immediately before calling irq_set_affinity; and aborts on mismatch. This makes userspace writes, irqbalance moves, or driver affinity updates win under races. If irq_set_affinity returns an error or if the resulting mask is truncated by core or irqchip constraints, the controller records the returned state, increments a rejection counter, and enters exponential backoff until new evidence or a generation change arrives. 8. Expose only aggregated counters and only to privileged users. There is no userspace PEBS record export and no unprivileged perf participation. Enablement is restricted to CAP_SYS_ADMIN or a boot or sysctl policy that already allows kernel profiling. DS area and PEBS buffers remain kernel-owned; exports are limited to aggregated per-IRQ counters such as current preferred CPU set, EWMA cost, loss counts, overflow counts, and last rejection reason. 9. Keep unsupported systems safe. If the CPU lacks required PEBS capabilities, if kernel PMU collection is disallowed, or if the IRQ fails the eligibility filter, the mechanism simply does not publish cookies or actuate affinity. The existing IRQ behavior remains unchanged.

2.6 Validation Plan

Correctness validation has four layers. A. Attribution correctness. Use synthetic nested-interrupt tests to prove stack semantics: device IRQ A interrupted by device IRQ B, plus PEBS PMI arrival during both windows. Confirm that samples taken while B is active are attributed only to B, that A resumes attribution after B exits, and that any stack-overflow case results in counted drops rather than silent misattribution. Verify that entry and exit publication bounds the attribution interval by checking seqcount live-bit transitions against traceable timestamps. B. Identity safety and lifecycle races. Stress request_irq, free_irq, shared-action insertion and removal, irq-domain deactivate or free, and CPU hotplug while PMIs continue arriving. Confirm that samples with stale config_gen are discarded, that no desc or action pointers are dereferenced from PMI, and that recycled virq numbers cannot inherit old evidence. Add dedicated tests for transition from single-action to shared-action and back, because that is the key mixed-action ambiguity boundary. C. Policy and actuation safety. Exercise managed MSI-X vectors, explicit administrator affinity writes, irqbalance interference, nohz_full or isolcpus housekeeping configurations, and hotplug offlining of the currently preferred CPU. Confirm that every applied mask is a subset of the current allowed envelope, that external affinity writes reset the controller baseline, and that repeated core or irqchip rejections push the controller into backoff rather than oscillation. Validate that Intel interrupt-remapping systems show ordinary IRTE updates through existing paths and that the invention never programs IRTEs directly. D. Performance and stability. Measure hot-path overhead with the feature disabled, observe-only, and fully enabled. The target is bounded per-CPU publication cost only: one predictable branch and one seqcount-protected push or pop on eligible interrupts, with no allocations and no cross-CPU cacheline bouncing. Measure NMI handler overhead under ring pressure and confirm loss accounting rather than stalls. Benchmark tail latency, LLC miss behavior, and memory-stall cycles on representative NVMe, NIC, and virtualized host workloads to show that cost-aware placement outperforms rate-only balancing while preserving correctness. Representative test assets include kselftest coverage for generation and hotplug races, perf-assisted kernel selftests for PEBS gating, and trace cross-checks that compare cookie live intervals with IRQ handler entry or exit tracepoints during debug builds.

4. Risks & Mitigations

  • Risk: Shared IRQs and threaded interrupts create attribution ambiguity. Mitigation: define control eligibility as exactly one primary hardirq action and exclude shared, chained, per-CPU, NMI, IPI, threaded-only, and wake-thread-only cases; optionally keep them observe-disabled rather than guessing.
  • Risk: Recycled virq numbers or freed descriptors could cause stale evidence to drive a new IRQ instance. Mitigation: the PMI path never stores desc or action pointers, and every sample key contains config_gen that is incremented on setup, free, sharing-state transitions, and domain teardown; mismatched generations are dropped before aggregation and before actuation.
  • Risk: Nested interrupts and PMI-on-hardirq can misattribute samples. Mitigation: use a bounded per-CPU stack instead of overwrite semantics, protect publication with seqcount, and drop rather than guess on overflow or unstable reads.
  • Risk: Controller oscillation or fights with userspace, irqbalance, or driver affinity changes. Mitigation: require improvement hysteresis and minimum dwell time, revalidate generations before every apply, make external writers win, and back off after rejection or truncation.
  • Risk: Violation of managed affinity, housekeeping, hotplug, or remapping constraints. Mitigation: compute every requested mask as a subset of the current managed or administrator envelope intersected with housekeeping and online CPUs, and actuate only through irq_set_affinity so the existing core, irqchip, and Intel IOMMU paths retain full authority.
  • Risk: Security or information leak through PEBS data. Mitigation: use kernel-owned PEBS buffers only, restrict enablement and visibility to privileged administration, export only aggregated counters, and never expose raw per-sample PEBS or LBR records to unprivileged users.
  • Risk: Hot-path overhead on high-rate interrupts. Mitigation: perform only per-CPU publication on eligible interrupts, avoid allocations and global locks, avoid cacheline bouncing, keep unsupported or ineligible cases on a fast no-op branch, and let the heavier aggregation and policy work run in process context.
